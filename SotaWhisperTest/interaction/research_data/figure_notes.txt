================================================================================
  PRESENTATION NOTES — Figure 1 to Figure 10
  Study: Effect of Robot Memory (Reciprocity) on Human-Robot Interaction
================================================================================

Background for all figures:
- Study uses a 2x2 mixed design: Between-subjects factor = Group (G1 Treatment
  vs G2 Control), Within-subjects factor = Session (S1 vs S2).
- G1 Treatment (n=5): P01, P03, P05, P07, P09 — robot REMEMBERS user in S2
- G2 Control (n=4): P02, P04, P06, P08 — robot PRETENDS TO FORGET in S2
- Session 1 (Day 1): Both groups have identical "novice" experience (baseline)
- Session 2 (Day 2+): Manipulation occurs — G1 gets personalized recall, G2
  gets stranger-like interaction again
- Questionnaire uses 7-point Likert scale (1=Strongly Disagree, 7=Strongly Agree)
- 5 subscales measured: Trust, Disclosure, Empathy, Robot Character, Continuity

================================================================================
SUMMARY — THE OVERALL STORY (for your conclusion slide)
================================================================================

1. DESIGN (Fig 1-3): A carefully controlled experiment where both groups start
   with identical baseline experiences. The only manipulation is whether the
   robot shows it remembers the user in Session 2.

2. BEHAVIORAL EVIDENCE (Fig 4-5): Treatment participants talked 4x longer,
   took 5x more turns, and used 2.4x more words per turn. Every single
   Treatment participant increased engagement. Control participants showed
   no improvement or slight decline.

3. SUBJECTIVE EVIDENCE (Fig 6-10): Treatment participants rated the robot
   higher on ALL five subscales (Trust, Disclosure, Empathy, Robot Character,
   Continuity). Control participants' ratings declined, especially on Trust
   and Empathy. The effects are consistent across individuals, not driven
   by outliers.

4. TAKEAWAY: Robot memory/reciprocity is not a "nice to have" — it actively
   builds rapport and engagement. Conversely, failing to remember actively
   damages the relationship. For social robots, memory is fundamental to
   meaningful interaction.

================================================================================
Q1: "What is the objective/goal of this research?"
================================================================================

Short answer:
  To empirically test whether a social robot that remembers past interactions
  (memory-based reciprocity) can increase user trust, self-disclosure, and
  emotional connection — compared to a robot that treats every meeting as new.

Full answer:
  Social robots are increasingly used in healthcare, education, and
  companionship. But most current robots have no persistent memory — every
  interaction starts from zero. Imagine talking to someone every day and they
  never remember your name or what you discussed. That's how most social
  robots work today.

  This research asks a simple but important question: if we give the robot
  memory — so it remembers your name, what you talked about, and greets you
  personally — does that change how people feel about the robot?

  We designed a controlled experiment where one group gets a robot that
  remembers them (Treatment), and one group gets a robot that pretends to
  forget (Control). Both groups start with the same baseline experience. Then
  we measure the difference — both in actual behavior (how long they talk,
  how many turns) and in subjective ratings (trust, disclosure, empathy,
  robot character, continuity).

  The goal is to isolate MEMORY-BASED RECOGNITION as a specific mechanism,
  separate from other factors like familiarity through repeated exposure.


================================================================================
Q2: "What is the urgency and contribution of this research?"
================================================================================

Short answer:
  There is a clear research gap: no prior study has empirically isolated
  robot memory-based recognition as a reciprocity mechanism in multi-session
  HRI. This study fills that gap with a novel LLM + persistent memory system.

Full answer — why this matters NOW:

  1. RESEARCH GAP (theoretical contribution):
     - Most HRI studies use single-session designs (Abdi et al., 2024). They
       only measure first impressions. But real relationships develop over
       multiple interactions — and memory is central to that.
     - Existing multi-session studies (Irfan et al., 2023; Churamani et al.,
       2024) focus on general personalization, but none have ISOLATED memory-
       based recognition as the specific variable being tested.
     - Smedegaard et al. (2025) note that "familiarity through exposure" and
       "familiarity through recognition" are different things — but no one has
       tested this distinction empirically. Our study does exactly that: the
       Control group gets exposure (they meet the robot twice) but NOT
       recognition (the robot pretends to forget). This lets us separate the
       two effects.

  2. TECHNICAL CONTRIBUTION:
     - This is one of the first studies to integrate an LLM (Ollama/Llama)
       with persistent user memory for multi-session social robot interaction.
     - The system architecture (Whisper STT + LLM + face recognition +
       persistent memory + FSM) is a working prototype that can be replicated
       and extended by other researchers.
     - The study demonstrates that a SMALL technical change (loading a user
       profile into the LLM prompt) produces a LARGE behavioral effect (4x
       engagement increase). This is a practical, implementable finding.

  3. SOCIETAL URGENCY:
     - Social robots are being deployed in elderly care, therapy, and
       education. If these robots cannot remember the people they serve, they
       risk being perceived as cold and mechanical — reducing trust and
       willingness to engage.
     - Our data shows that forgetting doesn't just fail to build rapport — it
       ACTIVELY DAMAGES the relationship (Control group trust dropped -0.80).
       This means deploying memoryless social robots may be counterproductive.
     - As LLMs become more common in robotics, understanding how to use memory
       effectively is critical for the next generation of social robots.


================================================================================
Q3: "Is the conclusion just 'the remembered group went up 4x'? So what?"
================================================================================

Short answer:
  No. The 4x is the surface finding. The deeper conclusions are about WHY
  memory matters, the BIDIRECTIONAL effect, and the MECHANISM of reciprocity.

Full answer — the conclusion has 4 layers:

  LAYER 1 — The obvious finding (what happened):
  Yes, the Remember group talked 4.2x longer and rated the robot higher on
  all 5 subscales. But that's just the "what."

  LAYER 2 — The bidirectional effect (the surprising finding):
  The Control group didn't just stay flat — their ratings WENT DOWN. Trust
  dropped -0.80 (the largest single change in the entire dataset). This means
  forgetting is not neutral. A robot that forgets you is WORSE than a robot
  you've never met. This is a new finding — previous studies assumed that
  the worst case of no-memory is simply "no improvement." Our data suggests
  it's actually "active damage."

  Why? Because in Session 1, both groups met the robot for the first time.
  The interaction created an expectation — "I told this robot my name and
  where I'm from." When the Control group returned and the robot didn't
  acknowledge this, it violated the social norm of reciprocity. The user
  "gave" personal information, but the robot didn't "give back" recognition.
  This violation feels worse than never having the expectation at all.

  LAYER 3 — The mechanism (why it works — reciprocity theory):
  The results support reciprocity theory (Gouldner, 1960) applied to HRI:
  - The robot "gives" by remembering (showing that the user's words mattered).
  - The user "gives back" by engaging more (longer conversations, more turns,
    higher ratings).
  - This creates a positive feedback loop: memory → engagement → richer
    conversation → more to remember → stronger memory → even more engagement.

  This also supports the Computers Are Social Actors (CASA) paradigm (Nass &
  Moon, 2000) — people applied the social norm of reciprocity to the robot
  just as they would to another human.

  LAYER 4 — The practical design principle:
  For social robot designers, the implication is clear: PERSISTENT MEMORY IS
  NOT OPTIONAL. It is a fundamental requirement for meaningful HRI. And the
  technical cost is minimal — the only difference between our two conditions
  was loading a JSON user profile and adjusting one LLM system prompt. This
  small change produced the largest effect in the entire study.

  Concise version (if asked to summarize in one sentence):
  "Robot memory triggers social reciprocity — users who feel remembered
  reciprocate with deeper engagement and trust, while users who feel
  forgotten withdraw. Memory is the minimum viable feature for social robots."


================================================================================
Q4: "Your sample is only N=9, how can you draw conclusions?"
================================================================================

Short answer:
  We acknowledge the small sample. These are preliminary findings. However,
  the consistency of the effect across individuals and the convergence of
  behavioral + subjective data strengthen our confidence.

Full answer:
  You are right — N=9 (effective S2 questionnaire: G1=3, G2=3) is below the
  target of N=20. We cannot claim statistical significance with traditional
  thresholds. However:

  1. EFFECT SIZE over p-value: With small samples, effect sizes (Cohen's d)
     are more informative than p-values. The differences we observe are large
     — G1 duration went from 184s to 772s (4.2x), which is not a subtle
     effect that requires a large sample to detect.

  2. INDIVIDUAL CONSISTENCY: Figure 9 (slope chart) shows that EVERY G1
     participant increased on most subscales, and EVERY G2 participant
     decreased or stayed flat. The effect is not driven by one outlier — it's
     consistent across all individuals. This is rare and meaningful.

  3. CONVERGENCE: Behavioral data (objective, from logs) and questionnaire
     data (subjective, self-report) tell the same story independently. When
     two different measurement methods agree, it strengthens confidence even
     with small N.

  4. This is a PILOT study. The goal at this stage is to establish whether
     the effect EXISTS and whether the experimental design works — both of
     which are demonstrated. The next step is to scale to N=20+ to confirm
     with statistical power.


================================================================================
Q5: "Why did you use a mixed design instead of fully between-subjects?"
================================================================================

Short answer:
  Because the research question is fundamentally about CHANGE over time —
  does the robot remembering you change the relationship from Session 1 to
  Session 2? A pure between-subjects design cannot capture within-person
  change.

Full answer:
  The mixed design (between: Group, within: Session) gives us two advantages:

  1. BASELINE CONTROL: By measuring each participant in both S1 and S2, we
     can see their personal baseline (S1) and their response to the
     manipulation (S2). This controls for individual differences — some
     people are naturally more trusting or talkative, and the within-subjects
     factor accounts for this.

  2. ISOLATING THE MANIPULATION: In Session 1, both groups get the identical
     "Novice" experience. Any differences in S2 can be attributed to the
     manipulation (Remember vs No-Remember), not to pre-existing group
     differences.

  A fully between-subjects design would only compare G1-S2 vs G2-S2 without
  knowing each person's baseline, making it harder to determine whether
  differences are due to the manipulation or to individual variation.

Q6: "Is the control condition fair? The robot pretends to forget — isn't
     that different from truly having no memory?"
================================================================================

Short answer:
  Yes, this was a deliberate design choice. The WOR condition still loads the
  user profile internally but acts as if it forgot. This ensures both
  conditions have the same INFORMATION but differ in BEHAVIOR.

Full answer:
  This is actually a strength of the design, not a weakness. As shown in
  Figure 3:

  - If we made the control condition truly memoryless (no profile loaded at
    all), the conversations would differ in CONTENT — the robot wouldn't know
    what topics to discuss, making the two conditions incomparable.
  - By having WOR load the profile but pretend to forget, we ensure:
      * Same topic availability (robot can still ask about Indonesia, studies)
      * Same conversation depth potential
      * The ONLY difference is the reciprocity signal: greeting by name,
        referencing past conversation, progressive social state.
  - This isolates the PERCEPTION of being remembered — which is exactly what
    reciprocity theory predicts matters.

  The red box at the bottom of Figure 3 explains this: "WOR still loads the
  user profile (knows origin) but acts like it forgot. This ensures the
  control condition has the same topic context but without the reciprocal
  memory signal — isolating the effect of recognition."


================================================================================
Q7: "What makes this different from existing personalization studies?"
================================================================================

Short answer:
  Most personalization studies change MULTIPLE things at once (conversation
  style, topics, difficulty level, etc.). Our study changes ONLY ONE thing:
  whether the robot shows it remembers you. This is a cleaner test of the
  memory/reciprocity mechanism.

Full answer:
  Existing work on robot personalization (Irfan et al., 2023; Churamani et
  al., 2024) typically involves adapting the robot's behavior based on user
  preferences — different topics, different difficulty levels, different
  communication styles. These studies show that personalization helps, but
  they cannot tell you WHICH ASPECT of personalization is responsible.

  Our study strips personalization down to its most basic form: RECOGNITION.
  Does the robot know who you are? Does it acknowledge your shared history?
  The conversation content, robot behavior, and system architecture are
  identical between conditions — only the memory signal differs.

  Additionally, we are among the first to:
  - Use an LLM (Llama 3.2) as the conversation engine with persistent memory
  - Test the effect across multiple interaction dimensions simultaneously
    (trust, disclosure, empathy, robot character, continuity)
  - Show the NEGATIVE effect of forgetting (not just the positive effect of
    remembering)

Q8: "The robot uses Japanese TTS for English — doesn't that affect results?"
================================================================================

Short answer:
  Yes, it affects both groups equally. The unnatural pronunciation may have
  suppressed Robot Character and Empathy scores for everyone, but it does not
  confound the GROUP comparison because both groups heard the same TTS.

Full answer:
  Sota uses TextToSpeechSota, a Japanese text-to-speech engine. When speaking
  English, it produces unnatural pronunciation with a Japanese accent. This
  is a limitation that likely:
  - Reduced perceived naturalness (Robot Character scores) for both groups
  - Made emotional expression harder to perceive (Empathy scores)
  - Created a "ceiling effect" — scores might be higher with proper English TTS

  However, this is a CONSTANT factor across both conditions. Both G1 and G2
  heard the same TTS quality. So while it may have suppressed absolute scores,
  it does NOT explain the DIFFERENCE between groups. If anything, it makes
  our findings more conservative — the effect of memory is strong enough to
  appear even with suboptimal TTS.

  Future work: replace with English-native TTS (e.g., OpenAI TTS, Google TTS)
  to test whether the memory effect becomes even stronger with natural speech.


================================================================================
Q9: "What about the latency problem? Does it confound your results?"
================================================================================

Short answer:
  Same as TTS — latency affects both groups equally. It is a limitation for
  absolute scores but not for the between-group comparison.

Full answer:
  The pipeline (record audio → Whisper STT on laptop → Ollama LLM generation
  → TTS → robot speaks) introduces several seconds of delay. P05 specifically
  noted: "I need to wait too long to talk to Sota, this kills the
  conversation flow."

  This latency:
  - Makes the conversation feel less natural for BOTH groups
  - May reduce engagement ceiling for BOTH groups
  - Is consistent across conditions (same pipeline)

  Interestingly, despite the latency, the Remember group STILL talked 4.2x
  longer. This suggests the memory effect is powerful enough to overcome
  technical friction. With reduced latency, the effect might be even larger.

Q10: "P08 seems like an outlier — are your results driven by this one person?"
================================================================================

Short answer:
  No. P08 has low scores, but the pattern holds even without P08. More
  importantly, P08's response is CONSISTENT with the theory — being forgotten
  is expected to produce negative reactions.

Full answer:
  P08 (G2 Control) had notably low scores across most subscales, especially
  in S2. Their open-ended response was: "slow response and asking same
  question again and again... I don't feel any [emotion]... it's just robot."

  However:
  1. P08's S1 scores were ALREADY the lowest in the sample (Overall: 2.48).
     So their low S2 scores partly reflect their general baseline, not just
     the manipulation.
  2. Even excluding P08, G2's other participants (P02, P06) still showed flat
     or declining scores from S1 to S2 — the pattern holds.
  3. Looking at Figure 9 (slope chart), the upward trend for G1 is visible
     across ALL G1 participants (P01, P05, P07), not just in contrast to P08.
  4. P08's strong negative reaction is actually PREDICTED by reciprocity
     theory — someone who gave personal information and got nothing back
     should feel the violation most strongly if they are already less trusting.

Q11: "Can you really apply reciprocity theory to a robot? It's not human."
================================================================================

Short answer:
  Yes. The CASA (Computers Are Social Actors) paradigm by Nass & Moon (2000)
  established that people apply social norms — including reciprocity — to
  computers and robots. Our results provide new evidence for this.

Full answer:
  This is a foundational question in HRI. The CASA paradigm (Nass & Moon,
  2000; Reeves & Nass, 1996) demonstrated through extensive experiments that
  people unconsciously apply social rules to machines — including politeness,
  reciprocity, gender stereotypes, and in-group/out-group bias.

  Our study adds NEW evidence to this framework:
  - When the robot "gave" by remembering (a social act), participants "gave
    back" by engaging more and rating it higher.
  - When the robot "took" by forgetting (a social violation), participants
    "withdrew" by reducing engagement and trust.
  - The bidirectional effect (remember → positive, forget → negative) is
    exactly what reciprocity theory predicts for human-human interaction.

  The fact that participants responded this way to a small 28cm robot with
  Japanese TTS and visible latency makes the finding even more compelling —
  the reciprocity response is robust enough to occur even in clearly non-
  human conditions.

Q12: "What is the practical application of this research?"
================================================================================

Short answer:
  Social robots deployed in real settings (elderly care, therapy, education)
  should implement persistent user memory as a MINIMUM requirement.

Full answer:
  1. ELDERLY CARE ROBOTS: Robots that visit elderly people regularly should
     remember their names, family stories, health topics, and preferences.
     Our data shows this simple feature dramatically increases willingness to
     engage — critical for combating loneliness.

  2. THERAPEUTIC ROBOTS: In mental health applications, trust and self-
     disclosure are essential. A robot that remembers past sessions can build
     therapeutic rapport progressively, just like a human therapist.

  3. EDUCATIONAL ROBOTS: A tutoring robot that remembers a student's progress,
     interests, and struggles can provide more meaningful interaction. Our
     Continuity subscale (+0.81) shows people WANT to keep interacting when
     remembered.

  4. DESIGN GUIDELINE: The technical cost is minimal — our implementation
     required only a JSON user profile and an adjusted LLM system prompt. Any
     robot with LLM integration can implement this. The ROI is enormous: small
     memory, big impact.

  5. WARNING: Deploying social robots WITHOUT memory may be actively harmful
     to user relationships (Control group trust -0.80). Designers should treat
     memory as a requirement, not an optional feature.
